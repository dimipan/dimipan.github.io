The Men Who Built the Cages

Creon, Torvald, and the tragedy of the frightened king.

On Sophocles, Euripides, Ibsen, Strindberg — and a single disease, diagnosed across twenty-four centuries.

≈ 26 min readBy Dimitris & Ananya

A Different Way of Looking

We have spent two essays so far looking at the women of these plays — the ones who walked out, the ones who stayed in and burned, the ones who weaponised the cage from inside it, the ones who let it close on them and called the closing freedom. We did this on purpose. In any conversation about Antigone or A Doll's House or Medea, the heroine is the one we run toward first, because she is the one whose pain is most legible and whose courage is most visible. She is doing the dramatic thing. Of course we look at her.

But there is a problem with looking only at her, and it is a problem the playwrights themselves did not have. Sophocles did not write a play called Antigone in order to celebrate Antigone. He wrote a play in which Antigone and Creon collide, and the whole play is the collision, and at the end of the collision the one left alive is Creon, kneeling among corpses, asking the gods to take him too. Ibsen did not write A Doll's House and stop the action at Nora's exit. The very last beat of the play — the last sound on the stage before the door closes from below — is Torvald, alone in the room, his face in his hands, whispering the words the most wonderful thing of all. The playwrights, more often than not, leave the heroine offstage in the final image and leave the camera fixed on the man she has just outgrown.

We think this is not an accident, and we think the men deserve their own essay.

Not a sympathetic essay, exactly — and certainly not an exculpating one. But a clinical one. Because what becomes clear, when you put Creon next to Torvald next to Jason next to Theseus next to the absent Count of Miss Julie, is that the playwrights are diagnosing the same disease in five different bodies, across twenty-four centuries, and that the disease is more interesting and more useful than any individual case of it. These men are not different villains. They are the same man in different costumes. And the disease they share, once you can see it, turns out to be one of the most precisely observed pathologies in Western drama.

It is also, we will say plainly, a disease still circulating.

So this is the third essay in our series, and it is the men's chapter. We will look at five of them — Creon in Antigone, Jason in Medea, Theseus in Hippolytus, Torvald in A Doll's House, and the Count in Miss Julie (who, brilliantly, never even appears on stage). We will try to name the disease they share. And we will try to honour what the playwrights, almost unanimously, do at the end — which is not to defeat these men but to pity them.

The First Symptom: They Are Not Acting Out of Hatred

The first thing to notice — and it is the thing that makes everything else fall into place — is that none of these men hates the woman in front of him. None of them is what we would now call a misogynist in the active, ideological sense. None of them is plotting against her in the way a real villain would plot. If you accused any of them, in the moment, of being cruel to the woman beside him, he would be genuinely offended. He believes he is doing the right thing. Often he believes he is doing it out of love.

This is what makes them so terrifying, and it is what makes them so much more historically accurate than a simple villain would be.

Torvald is the clearest case, because Ibsen has put us inside the marriage for three days and we have heard the cooing endearments and the playful petting and the tender little prohibitions on macaroons. Torvald believes he loves Nora. He says so, repeatedly, and he is not lying. The problem is that what he calls love is a structure of small, kindly diminishments — little squirrel, little songbird, little featherbrain — designed, without him quite noticing, to keep her permanently below him. When the blackmail letter arrives and he turns on her, his first cry is not how could you do this but I am in the power of an unscrupulous man because of a thoughtless woman. The grammar is doing all the work. He is not angry that Nora forged the bond. He is angry that her thoughtlessness has placed him in a humiliating position. The wife is not a person whose moral situation he can imagine; she is a feature of his social standing, and the feature has malfunctioned. And when the second letter arrives and the danger evaporates, Torvald immediately switches back to loving mode: of course I forgive you, men love to forgive, you must lean on me, I shall be your conscience and your will. He genuinely believes this is generosity. He does not see — he is constitutionally unable to see — that what he is offering is not love but a return to the only mode in which he knows how to feel safe, which is the mode in which he is large and Nora is small.

My partner, in our notes, found the cleanest sentence anyone has written about Torvald, and I will translate it again because it is the foundation of everything that follows in this essay:

Torvald is the tragedy. The biggest problem is that he believes he loves Nora, in his own distorted way. And that makes it worse. He cannot see the cage, because he built it out of materials he himself considered to be care and love. He confuses possession with protection, guidance with tenderness, and moral superiority with devotion.

Read that again, slowly. He cannot see the cage, because he built it out of materials he himself considered to be care and love. This is the diagnosis. The bars are real, the door is locked, the bird inside is wilting — but the man holding the key has labelled every bar with the word love, and as far as he is concerned the labelling is the truth.

Now look at Creon. Antigone is a different play in every external respect — a king instead of a banker, a corpse instead of a forged signature, a death sentence instead of a marital disagreement — and yet Creon's first speech to the elders of Thebes is a long, sincere, almost moving address about how the city must come first and how a ruler must place public welfare above private affection. He is not a tyrant in the way a Roman emperor is a tyrant. He is a patriot. He believes, completely, that he is doing the harder, lonelier thing — that to leave Polynices unburied is a painful but necessary act for the safety of Thebes. Antigone has, in his understanding, attacked not him personally but the city itself, the public good, the order on which everyone's lives depend. When he refuses to relent even after his son Haemon and the prophet Teiresias have warned him, he is not being cruel for the pleasure of cruelty. He is being, in his own mind, a serious man, a man who does not bend to private feeling. He is doing what he believes the office of king requires.

And Jason — Jason, perhaps most galling of all — explicitly tells Medea that his new royal marriage is for her benefit. He has worked it out. By marrying the princess of Corinth, he can secure royal connections, social standing, a future for his sons. Medea should be grateful. She is, he gently explains, being too emotional. She is failing to see the long-term picture. He is not abandoning her; he is providing for her. He is, in his own understanding, the responsible adult in the room.

Each of these men has constructed an elaborate inner narrative in which he is the hero. He is the protector. He is the rational one. He is the one carrying the burden of public duty or moral seriousness or rational planning, while the woman beside him collapses into emotion or piety or hysteria. None of them sees himself as the antagonist. That is the thing the playwrights insist we understand first. Tyranny that calls itself tyranny is comparatively easy to resist. Tyranny that calls itself love, or duty, or rational planning is much harder to escape, because the people who build it cannot, even in principle, see what they are doing.

The Second Symptom: It Is Not About Her, It Is About Him

Once you have noticed that none of these men is consciously cruel, you begin to notice the second thing, which is that almost none of what they do is actually about the woman in question. The crisis of the play, in each case, is presented as a conflict between the man and the woman; but if you look more carefully at the man's actual fears, you discover that the woman is barely in them. The fear is about something else, almost always the same something else: what other men will think of him.

Torvald, again, is the cleanest case. When he reads the blackmail letter, his first thoughts are not about Nora. They are about his promotion to bank manager. They are about the gossip that will reach his colleagues. They are about his humiliation, his exposure, his destroyed future. I am in the power of an unscrupulous man because of a thoughtless woman. Notice the word I. The whole sentence is about Torvald's standing in the male world of Christiania banking. Nora's actual situation — a woman who forged a signature to save her husband's life and has been quietly paying off the loan ever since at the cost of her own peace — does not enter his calculation at all. She is the cause of his predicament; she is not herself a person with a predicament. And when the danger evaporates, the forgiveness he extends is not really about her either. It is about the relief of his own reputation. He can afford to be magnanimous because his social standing is intact again.

Creon shows the same pattern, and Sophocles is even more savage about it. The deepest layer of the Antigone is not the conflict between divine law and civil law. It is the fact that Creon, behind all his dignified speeches about the city, is terrified of being humiliated by a woman. He says so. He says so in lines that make modern audiences laugh in disbelief because they are so astonishingly naked. I am no man and she the man instead, he cries, if she can have this conquest without pain. No woman shall be the master while I live. This is not the language of a civic statesman. This is the language of a man who has discovered, to his horror, that the universe might be arranged in a way that lets a girl be right and a king be wrong, and who would rather destroy Thebes than allow that arrangement to stand. The corpse on the plain is a pretext. The unburied brother is a pretext. What Creon is actually defending — at the cost of his son, his wife, his reign — is the position of the king as the kind of being who cannot be publicly contradicted by a woman. It is the most expensive vanity in literature.

Jason is the same disease in the more sophisticated form of self-deception. His public reason for the new marriage is dynastic strategy. His private reason — the one Euripides forces him, eventually, to half-confess — is that he wants the prestige of being the king's son-in-law. He wants to walk into the assembly and have the other men nod to him in a different way. Medea, who has saved his life, betrayed her father, killed her brother, and crossed the sea for him, is no longer useful for this purpose. A foreign sorceress is not an asset at a Corinthian dinner table. The princess is. And so Medea is to be, in his elegant phrasing, practical about this. The marriage was never about her. It was never even about love. It was about Jason's place in the room of other men, and when a better placement became available he took it without noticing he had done anything wrong.

And Theseus — Theseus is perhaps the most chilling of the group, because we see his disease not in domestic detail but in a single decisive act. He returns from his travels to find his wife Phaedra dead by her own hand, with a tablet in her fingers accusing his son Hippolytus of rape. The accusation is a lie. Phaedra wrote it to protect her own name after her secret love for Hippolytus had been exposed. But Theseus does not pause. He does not investigate. He does not summon Hippolytus and listen. He calls down upon his son one of the three curses Poseidon has granted him, and Hippolytus is dragged to his death by a sea-monster on the road out of Trozen. Why is Theseus so quick to believe the tablet? Because the tablet is written in the voice of a dead, honourable wife, and a dead honourable wife is, in his world, an unimpeachable witness against a living son. The cage of patriarchal honour has worked perfectly: the dead wife's reputation has been preserved, the bastard son has been destroyed, and Theseus has done what the script of his role requires. He is not a malicious man. He is a man who has so completely accepted the conventions of his world that the conventions can be used as a weapon against him without his ever noticing he has been holding the weapon. Phaedra has not so much killed Hippolytus as let the patriarchy do it, and Theseus is the patriarchy's agent. He is not even, in any meaningful sense, a participant. He is a function the system has invoked.

The Count, in Miss Julie, is the same idea pushed to a kind of theatrical limit. Strindberg makes the brilliant decision never to put him on stage. He is present only as a pair of large boots placed at the side of the kitchen and a bell that rings whenever someone calls. The boots and the bell. That is what the patriarchal authority of the household consists of, in Strindberg's terrifying analysis: a pair of empty boots and the threat of being summoned by a sound. Julie is destroyed not by anything her father actually does, but by the prospect of his eventual return and the discovery of her degradation. The Count does not need to act. He only needs to exist, and to be expected. The bell rings; Julie shudders; the razor is fetched; the play ends. The most powerful patriarch in our list is the one who never says a word, because he has trained his daughter so thoroughly to be afraid of his judgement that his actual presence is redundant.

So this is the second symptom. The man's experience of the crisis is not really about the woman at all. It is about his place among men, his reputation in the public world, his standing in a system whose conventions he has so completely internalised that he treats them as the order of nature. The woman is the inconvenient piece of evidence that the system is failing him. Her job — in his understanding — is to disappear back into compliance so that his status can be restored.

When she refuses to disappear, the crisis becomes total.

The Third Symptom: The Weaponising of Reason Against Feeling

The third symptom is the one that has aged perhaps most uncomfortably, because it is still in such heavy daily use. All of these men, in the moment of crisis, frame themselves as the voice of calm rational thought and frame the woman as the voice of irrational emotion. They do this not because it is true — in every case the woman is the one with the clearer head — but because the framing is a tool. If you can establish that you are reasonable and she is hysterical, you have already won the argument before the argument begins, because anything she says next can be filed under emotional and quietly ignored.

Watch each of them do it.

Torvald, throughout the play, treats Nora's intelligence as a charming impossibility. She is his little featherbrain, his little spendthrift, the dear creature who cannot possibly understand finance or moral seriousness or the way the world actually works. When he discovers that this little featherbrain has, in fact, single-handedly arranged a complex financial transaction, kept it secret for years, and managed the repayments out of her household allowance, his response is not to revise his estimate of her intelligence. His response is to be frightened by her competence and to recast it, immediately, as moral failure. The very fact that she could do this without him noticing becomes evidence that she is unfit to raise his children. The intelligence, the resourcefulness, the love that motivated the whole act — none of these is allowed to count. Only the framing remains: she is the foolish creature, he is the responsible adult, and any rearrangement of those positions is a violation of nature.

Jason does this with a masterclass in patronising rhetoric. His long speech to Medea after the betrayal is one of the most infuriating set-pieces in Greek drama, because Jason is genuinely trying to be nice. He explains, slowly, as if to a child, that her anger is misplaced, that he has been thinking about the family's long-term interests, that if she could just calm down she would see the wisdom of the new arrangement. He is the rational planner; she is the emotional foreigner. The framing is the whole point. By casting Medea as overwrought, he licenses himself to ignore everything she says — including her perfectly accurate observation that he is breaking a sacred oath and abandoning the woman who saved his life. Her arguments cannot land, in his understanding, because they are arriving from the wrong category of person.

Creon does the same thing with the language of statehood. He repeatedly contrasts his own sober public-spirited reasoning with what he treats as Antigone's irrational, almost feminine attachment to a dead body. Spiritual arguments — the unwritten laws, the rites of the dead — are, in his frame, things women do; political arguments — order, the security of Thebes, the rule of law — are what serious men do. The fact that Antigone's arguments are, by any honest measure, both more philosophically rigorous and more universally binding than his own is invisible to him, because he has already filed her under the emotional heading. When even his own son Haemon comes to him and explains, gently, that the city of Thebes is on Antigone's side, Creon's response is to accuse Haemon of being womanish — of having had his head turned by his fiancée. The framing has become so total that any disagreement, from anyone, gets re-coded as femininity and dismissed.

Theseus does not engage with Hippolytus' rationality at all. He gets a single speech of denial from his son and responds by exiling him and unleashing the curse. Reason, in this moment, is a thing he simply will not extend to his living child. The dead wife's tablet has framed the situation, and inside that frame Hippolytus is a defendant who can only be guilty.

And the Count of Miss Julie does not need to weaponise reason because the boots and the bell have already done the work. Julie is not arguing with him. She is collapsing in advance of his return, because she has so thoroughly absorbed the framing that she does not even imagine an alternative.

This is the third symptom and it is, in some ways, the most insidious of the three. The first two — I am acting out of love and this is really about my standing — are at least available to introspection if a man chose to do the work. The third — I am the rational one, she is the emotional one, therefore her arguments do not count — is built into the grammar of the language these men speak. It does the rhetorical work invisibly. It is what makes the cage feel, to the man holding the keys, like a feature of the world rather than a structure he is maintaining.

The Fourth Symptom: The Final Pity

Now look at how the playwrights end these stories.

This is, we think, the most important thing about the entire essay, and it is the part that is hardest to describe without sentimentality. Sophocles, Euripides, Ibsen, Strindberg — none of them is interested in defeating these men in the way a melodrama defeats its villain. None of them gives us the satisfaction of a triumphant comeuppance. What they do instead is something subtler and crueller and ultimately more truthful: they leave the men standing, stripped of the framework that gave their lives meaning, and they let us see what is left.

Look at Creon at the end of Antigone. He is alive. He is still the king. He has, technically, won the legal argument — the city has not rebelled, the corpse stayed unburied long enough for his decree to be honoured, his authority remains intact. But Antigone has hanged herself in the cave. His son Haemon has fallen on his own sword over her body. His wife Eurydice has killed herself in the palace. Creon walks back onto the stage carrying his son's corpse and is told, almost immediately, that his wife is dead too. He begs the gods to take him. They do not. He is sentenced to go on living — to continue being the king of a city he has emptied, to wear a crown that has cost him everything that gave the crown meaning. The messenger, in the closing lines of the play, has to tell him to stand up and take his ruined house inside. There is no triumph in this. There is no music. There is just the sight of a man who has finally understood, twenty pages too late, that the framework he was defending was not worth the things he sacrificed to defend it.

Look at Jason at the end of Medea. He stands on the stage screaming up at the dragon-chariot in which Medea is escaping with the bodies of their dead children. He has lost everything. The princess he was going to marry is dead, poisoned by Medea's gifts. Her father the king is dead, melted in the same poison. His sons — the dynastic future for which he discarded Medea in the first place — are dead by their mother's hand. Medea will not even let him bury them. I will bury them myself, she says, from above. He is a king of nothing; he is, by the end, not even a father. Euripides gives him the pure spectacle of his ruin. He does not give him understanding, exactly — Jason continues, to the end, to misunderstand what has happened to him — but he gives us, the audience, the unmistakable image of a man who built his life around the wrong things and now has nothing left.

Look at Torvald at the end of A Doll's House. The stage direction is one of the most famous in modern drama: He sinks down on a chair near the door and buries his face in his hands. He whispers Nora's name. He looks around at the empty room. Empty. And then, suddenly, almost involuntarily, the most wonderful thing of all? — a half-question reaching out toward the impossible, unspoken miracle in which they could become real to each other. Then the door is heard from below. The miracle is not arriving. The play ends. Ibsen does not give Torvald a moment of explicit understanding; what he gives him is the experience of the room becoming empty, the sudden discovery that the cage had a bird in it and the bird is gone and the cage is now just a room with furniture in it.

This is the playwrights' verdict on the men, and it is the same verdict in three different theatrical languages. They are not punished, exactly — Creon and Torvald are alive, Jason is alive — but they are exposed. The framework they built their lives around is shown to be, in the end, a frame around nothing. They had thought they were the masters of their houses. The playwrights show us, in the closing image, that they were merely the wardens, and that the prison was as much for them as for the women they were guarding. And, perhaps most painfully, that they did not even know they were prisoners until the door was opened from inside.

Sophocles does not hate Creon. He pities him. Ibsen does not hate Torvald. He pities him. Euripides does not hate Jason. He pities him.

The pity is the verdict. Hatred would have been a kind of mercy, because hatred would have given the men the dignity of being significant antagonists. Pity demotes them to what they actually are: small frightened men who confused control with respect, possession with protection, and the public role with the private soul.

The Disease, Named

We can now put a name on the thing the playwrights are all diagnosing.

Across all five men — Creon, Jason, Theseus, Torvald, the Count — the same syndrome shows up. We can describe it as cleanly as we know how.

These men have been raised in cultures that promised them mastery — over the household, the city, the family, the woman. The promise was made in the form of a script: be the head, be the protector, be the rational one, be the upholder of order, and the world will arrange itself around you in stable orbits. The script was given to them by their fathers and grandfathers and by the entire architecture of their society. It was not, in any moment, a thing they had to choose to believe, because it was the air they breathed.

The trouble with the script is that it was written to be performed in front of an audience of compliant women, and in each of these plays the woman has stopped being compliant. Antigone has buried her brother. Medea has refused to be discarded quietly. Phaedra — through her tablet — has revealed that even a dead wife can be a more disruptive force than a living son. Nora has done the calculation on the love offered to her and found it wanting. Julie has stepped off the pedestal her father built for her and discovered there was no floor underneath. In every case, the woman has done something the script does not contemplate, and the man, who has only the script, does not have the inner equipment to respond.

What he has instead is panic. And the panic is not — this is the diagnosis — panic about the woman. It is panic about what will happen to him if the script stops working. Because the script was the only structure he had. Underneath the script there is — he discovers, to his horror — no separately constructed self. There is no Torvald independent of Mr Helmer the bank manager. There is no Creon independent of the king who will not be defied by a woman. There is no Jason independent of the son-in-law of the king of Corinth. The patriarchal role is not a coat these men are wearing. It is the shape they are. Take away the role and there is no man underneath. There is just a frightened child who has been wearing a king for so long that he has forgotten he is a child.

This is the disease, as cleanly as we can state it: these are men who confused the role with the self, and who, when the role failed, had no self left.

Once you can see this, you can see why the playwrights' final image is so often the man alone in the room. The aloneness is not just dramatic; it is metaphysical. The room is empty because, in a sense, it has always been empty. The performance required an audience. Without the audience, there is nothing to perform, and without the performance, there is no performer. Creon kneeling among corpses. Jason screaming up at a dragon-chariot that will not bring him his children's bodies. Torvald with his face in his hands in a living room from which the woman has just removed herself. The Count returning home, in a sequel we never see, to a daughter who is now a corpse in the kitchen. These are images of the role without the audience, and the playwrights insist we understand that this is the truest picture of these men we have ever been given.

Why This Is Not a Period Piece

We have written this essay carefully, and you will have noticed that we have tried not to flatten the men into modern villains. They are not modern villains; they are figures from very specific historical situations — the royal absolutism of mythic Thebes, the aristocratic-bourgeois marriage of nineteenth-century Norway, the Swedish manor-house class system of 1888. The cages they built were the cages their cultures handed them. We are not in the business of judging the dead by laws they could not have known.

But we are also not going to pretend that what these men did has nothing to do with our world, because it does, and pretending otherwise would be a small dishonesty of the kind the men themselves were always committing.

The Torvalds are still here. They are not bank managers in 1879 Christiania any more, but the structure of I love you, I provide for you, therefore your independence is a personal injury to me is still being performed in marriages and friendships and workplaces all over the West, often by men who would describe themselves, sincerely, as feminists. The Creons are still here. They are not kings of Thebes, but the figure of the public man whose authority cannot tolerate being disagreed with by a woman — especially in front of other men — is one of the most reliable phenomena of contemporary politics, business, and academic life. We could give names. We choose not to. The point is not to score points off particular living people but to notice that the playwrights have given us an instrument for diagnosis, and that the instrument still produces readings on contemporary patients.

What is most painful, and most important, is that the disease still has its quiet form too. The man who is not Creon and not Torvald — who is, by all visible measures, kind, considerate, modern, progressive — can still confuse possession with protection without ever raising his voice. He can still confuse his own anxieties about his standing with concern for his partner's wellbeing. He can still frame himself as the rational one and his partner as the emotional one in a thousand small daily conversations, none of which would survive a transcript but all of which add up to the same architecture. The bars are quieter now. The bell is no longer literally rung. The boots are no longer literally placed. But the configuration is recognisable, and the playwrights have given us a vocabulary for noticing it.

My partner put it most precisely when she said, in our notes on A Doll's House:

Society built these men to believe they are masters of the world. But the playwrights strip them bare and reveal them as fragile, emotionally arrested children, who confuse control with respect.

That sentence is, in our reading, the closing diagnosis on Creon, Jason, Theseus, Torvald, and the Count. It is also, we suspect, a diagnosis a fair number of men today would, if they sat with it honestly, recognise in some part of themselves — and a fair number of women would recognise in men they have known. The disease has not been cured. It has only changed its costume.

A Final Word About Pity

We want to end where the playwrights end, which is not in anger and not in triumph but in pity, because pity is the most accurate emotional response to what is actually happening in these plays.

It is easy to read Antigone and want Creon punished. It is easy to read A Doll's House and want Torvald humiliated. It is easy, when one is angry, to want the men in these stories to suffer in proportion to what the women in them have suffered, and there is no shame in that anger; it is, in many places, an entirely correct anger. We do not want to talk anyone out of it.

But we want to point out that the playwrights themselves — who were closer to the cages, who had less of a vocabulary of feminist analysis to draw on, who in some cases personally benefited from the very systems they were exposing — chose pity. They chose pity over hatred. They chose to leave the men standing, alive, alone in the empty room, having discovered, too late, that the framework they sacrificed everything to defend was the framework that had been hollowing them out all along. The playwrights chose to show us that the man in the cage is also in the cage. He locked himself in along with the woman, because the cage was his only way of knowing who he was. When the woman walks out, what she leaves behind is not a tyrant in his fortress. What she leaves behind is a man who, for the first time in his life, has to find out whether there is anything inside him that is not the role.

Some of these men, in life, do find something. Some, presumably, do the work, undertake the painful demolition of the script, and rebuild themselves on something less brittle than an inherited part. We hope, in our better moments, that this is what most of them do. The playwrights, more pessimistically, do not show us the rebuilding. They show us only the moment of the empty room. They leave the rebuilding to the man — and to the world that produced him, and to the women who will not, any longer, be the audience for the performance.

There is, in the end, only one cure for the disease the playwrights diagnose, and it is the same cure available to everyone. A self has to be built that is not the role. A self that does not need to be the rational one, the head of the house, the king who cannot be contradicted, the husband who must be deferred to, in order to know that it is real. A self that can be, simply, a person — answerable to the same higher courts that Antigone and Nora and Socrates were answerable to, and not to the small applause of other men.

That is the rebuilding the playwrights leave to us.

Most of these stories end with the man alone in the empty room, beginning, perhaps, to do the work; or else refusing to do the work and simply continuing as a hollow shape. We do not, in the plays, find out which it will be. The plays end before we know.

But we know what is required. The playwrights have told us what is required. And in writing this last essay of the trilogy we have been trying, between the two of us, to honour what they told us — which is that the women in these plays were not the only ones in the cage, and that the men who built the cages were also, in their own much less visible way, the men who lived and died inside them.

Antigone closed her eyes in the cave.

Nora closed the door from below.

Creon, Jason, Torvald — they are still in the room.

They have, each of them, the rest of their lives to find out who they were before they were the part.

We hope — without much confidence, but with some — that some of them do.

Notes

Notes for the curious reader. This essay is the third in a sequence on what we have been calling, between us, the cage trilogy. The first essay traced four strategies of refusal among seven heroines — Antigone, Nora, Medea, Phaedra, Electra, Helen, and Miss Julie — and argued that only Antigone and Nora truly transcend the patriarchal cage rather than fighting from inside it. The second essay placed Antigone and Nora alongside Socrates as three figures who share a common structure of refusal across twenty-three centuries. This third essay turns the camera around and asks about the men. It draws on Sophocles' Antigone, Euripides' Medea and Hippolytus, Henrik Ibsen's A Doll's House, and August Strindberg's Miss Julie. The translated paragraphs in the central sections are again from notes my partner wrote in Greek, and the diagnostic framework — they confused the role with the self — is the result of a long, ongoing conversation between us. A future essay may turn to the enablers in these plays — the Nurses, the Jeans, the Krogstads — who make their living by knowing exactly how the cages work, and who profit, in their small ways, from helping them remain locked. But we will let this trilogy stand for a moment before we return.
