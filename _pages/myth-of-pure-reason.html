The Myth of Pure Reason

How to read the first philosophers without lying to yourself.

A reading of the Introduction to Robin Waterfield's The First Philosophers (Oxford World's Classics).

≈ 18 min readBy Dimitris & Ananya

Why This Book, Why This Introduction

There is a temptation, when you decide to take the history of Western thought seriously, to begin with the famous names. Plato. Aristotle. Descartes. Kant. The figures whose works survived intact, whose treatises sit on the shelf with confident spines and chapter headings.

I want to resist that temptation, and I want to explain why.

My reading plan is not linear. It is cyclical. I move between the philosophers and the poets, between the metaphysicians and the dramatists, because the line between literature and philosophy is much thinner than the academic departments would have you believe. To understand Kant, I will need to have read Hume; to understand Hume, I will eventually want to come back to Heraclitus; and to read any of them with real comprehension, I keep returning to Homer, to the Greek tragedians, and to Dostoevsky. The ideas pollinate each other across centuries. Cut one strand and the whole thing flattens into a textbook.

So when I picked up Robin Waterfield's edition of The First Philosophers: The Presocratics and Sophists, my goal was simple in stated form and vast in implication: I wanted to map the origins of two thinkers in particular — Parmenides, whose argument that reality is one and unchanging is the secret seed of Plato's Theory of Forms; and Heraclitus, whose vision of fire and flux is the secret seed of Nietzsche's revolt against Plato. The whole later quarrel of Western philosophy, in some sense, is already there in Ionia and southern Italy in the sixth century BCE.

But before you can read the Presocratics, you have to read about them. Their books did not survive. We have only fragments, often a single line at a time, embedded in the works of much later writers who quoted them for their own reasons. The man holding the tweezers matters as much as the fragment under glass. Waterfield is that man for this edition, and his Introduction is not a polite preface. It is a critical apparatus. It is the lens you must put on before you look at the specimen, because if you go in bare-eyed you will see exactly what every reader for two and a half millennia has been trained to see — and almost all of it is wrong.

What follows is my attempt to walk through Waterfield's Introduction the way I read it: not as a summary of his argument, but as a record of what it gave me, and what it took away.

A Romantic Lie

Waterfield opens with a poem. This is not an accident.

The last stanza of Friedrich Schiller's The Gods of Greece, written at the close of the eighteenth century, mourns the disappearance of the old pagan deities. The colours have gone home with them. The sounds of life have gone with them. What remains for us, the poet says, is das entseelte Wort — the de-souled Word.

That phrase is doing tremendous work. The "Word" here is logos: reason, argument, formula, the cold gleam of analysis. To call it de-souled is to claim that something terrible happened in human history. There was once a world in which mountains had spirits, in which the sea had a voice, in which thunder was the temper of a god. Then, at some point, men in long robes started measuring things and giving lectures, and the spirits left. We were given equations and lost the world.

It is a beautiful poem. It is also, Waterfield warns us, a beautiful trap.

Because the academic version of the history of philosophy tells the same story in dryer prose. The standard line — and you will find it in respectable textbooks — is that around the sixth century BCE, somewhere along the Ionian coast, a group of Greek thinkers suddenly invented science and philosophy. They turned away from myth. They turned toward reason. Mythos was replaced by logos. The clouds parted. Western thought began.

Both versions — the Romantic lament and the academic chronology — are telling the same story with different emotional colouring. One mourns the loss; the other celebrates the gain. But both assume the same clean break.

Waterfield does not believe this break ever happened.

To begin with, the Presocratics themselves were not the lab-coated rationalists the textbook suggests. Lump them all together as one tribe and you immediately do violence to the evidence. Empedocles wrote in verse, claimed to be a god, and may have thrown himself into a volcano; Waterfield is comfortable calling him a shaman. Heraclitus wrote in oracular fragments designed to wound the reader's certainty; Waterfield calls him a prophet. Pythagoras founded a religious cult that practised reincarnation and refused to eat beans. These are not the founders of the experimental method. These are mystics who happened to also reason.

To call the Presocratic moment the "birth of reason" is therefore to apply a category that did not exist for them, and to ignore everything that does not fit it. It is to read history backwards from a destination — modern science — that took two thousand years to arrive and that they would not have recognised as their own.

Worse, it is also to assume that what came before them was pure irrationality. This is patronising and it is false. Pre-philosophical societies were not stupid. They thought in metaphor, in analogy, in image and ritual — but those are systems of thought, not the absence of thought. The shift the Presocratics inaugurated was a change in the kind of explanation people found persuasive, not a change from no thinking to thinking.

Once you accept this, the whole Romantic frame collapses. There was no Eden of myth from which we were exiled by the cold word. There was no sudden dawn in which reason rose over a sleeping world. What there was — what is much more interesting — was a slow, uneven, partial, and never-completed shift in how human beings answered their oldest questions.

That is the first lens Waterfield hands you. The history of philosophy is not a march out of darkness. It is a dance.

The Distortion Game

Here is the practical problem.

The Presocratics did not leave us books. They left us, at most, a handful of lines apiece, sometimes a paragraph, occasionally — for Parmenides — most of a poem. Everything else we have was preserved by people writing centuries later, who quoted the early thinkers in passing while making arguments of their own. This tradition has a name: doxography, the writing-down of opinions. A doxographer is the philosophical equivalent of a court stenographer who is also one of the lawyers.

Two of those later writers matter more than any others, because almost everything we think we know about the first philosophers comes through them: Aristotle, and Plato.

And both of them were grinding axes.

Take Aristotle first. He is, in many ways, the reason we still have the Presocratics at all; without his discussions of his predecessors, vast tracts of early Greek thought would be lost completely. We owe him the kind of debt that is impossible to pay. But Waterfield is blunt about the cost: it has been established beyond serious dispute that Aristotle viewed the Presocratics almost entirely through the lens of his own philosophy.

What does this mean concretely? Aristotle had a theory of causation with four distinct kinds of cause — the material, the formal, the efficient, and the final. When he turned his gaze backward to Thales (who said everything is water) or to Anaximenes (who said everything is air), he assumed those thinkers were clumsily groping toward his own concept of the material cause. He boxed them up. He treated them as proto-Aristotelians who had not yet quite figured out the right answer — his answer. He even said, with no apparent embarrassment, that he was studying his predecessors mainly to shed light on his own theories.

So when you read a passage that says "Thales believed water is the principle of all things," you must ask yourself a hard question. Did Thales actually say that? Or are you reading Aristotle's translation of a thinker into vocabulary Thales never possessed, fitted into a framework Thales would not have recognised? The fragment is real. But the lens is Aristotle's, and the lens curves the light.

Plato played the same game with a different target. His campaign was not against the Presocratics, whose cosmological speculations he could absorb and remould; his campaign was against the Sophists. And the reason is not philosophical — it is personal.

Plato's mentor was Socrates. Socrates was put to death by Athens on charges that included corrupting the youth and impiety. Plato spent the rest of his career rehabilitating Socrates' reputation and, more importantly, distinguishing him from the men he was most likely to be confused with — the Sophists, the professional teachers of rhetoric and political skill who took fees from Athenian aristocrats. To save Socrates, Plato had to destroy them. He painted the Sophists as mercenary, as indifferent to truth, as morally hollow word-jugglers who would teach you to win an argument regardless of whether you were right. The portrait stuck. It is essentially the portrait we still carry around today; the word "sophistry" itself is the residue of Plato's smear.

But, Waterfield insists, it is a smear. The Sophists were not the cynical rhetoricians Plato painted. They were, in many cases, exactly the thinkers we should be reading if we want to understand the birth of moral and political philosophy in the West. And we will come back to them.

For now, take this away: when you read a fragment of a Presocratic, you are reading something that has passed through at least one filter, and very often two or three. Distortion was, in Waterfield's exact phrase, the name of the game. The historian's first task — and the reader's — is to remember that.

Proto-Scientists, Not Modern Scientists

If we cannot call the Presocratics the founders of myth-free reason, and we cannot trust the lens through which their successors recorded them, what can we say about them?

Were they at least scientists? The popular image — the one in those textbooks Waterfield distrusts — says yes: these were the first men to study the natural world systematically, the ancestors of Galileo and Newton.

Waterfield's answer is more careful. They were not scientists. But they invented something without which science could not later have happened — and that something is more important than the scientific method itself.

Consider what was missing. The Presocratics had no reliable concept of a controlled experiment. They did not test their hypotheses against systematic observation. When their theories ran into things they could see with their own eyes — and they often did — they typically did not change the theories. They did the opposite: they trusted the theory and discounted the observation. They were, as Waterfield puts it with admirable directness, dogmatists. Beautiful, brilliant, world-shifting dogmatists, but dogmatists.

So in what sense were they revolutionary?

To answer this, you have to understand what came before them. The Greek world they inherited from Homer and Hesiod operated on a principle we can call projection. When something awe-inspiring or terrifying happened — a storm, a drought, an earthquake, a strange dream — the Greek mind reached outward and found a god behind it. Poseidon was angry. Zeus was punishing. Apollo had withdrawn his favour. Every overwhelming feature of the natural world was treated as the moods of a person, often a person with a grudge.

Hesiod tried to bring some order to this enormous psychological export. He gave the gods family trees, organised them by domain, generated the world as a sequence of divine births and conflicts. But he never broke the basic spell. He remained a pluralist — he just built a tidier polytheism. The world was still being explained by an enormous cast of personalities.

The Presocratic move was a complete reversal of direction. Instead of projecting human feelings outward onto the world, they began looking inward — into the world itself — for an order that did not depend on anyone's mood. They asked what the world is made of. They asked whether all the apparent variety might be reducible to one or a few underlying principles. They proposed water, air, fire, an unbounded apeiron, atoms in a void. The specific answers do not matter for our purposes; what matters is the kind of question.

The kind of question carried two enormous, almost reckless assumptions inside it.

The first assumption was that the universe possesses an order — that beneath the chaos of appearances there is a logic, a kosmos in the original Greek sense, a beautiful arrangement waiting to be uncovered.

The second assumption — and this is the more vertiginous one — was that the human rational mind is the right tool to uncover it. Not prophecy. Not ritual. Not divine revelation given to a priest. Just a person, sitting and thinking, can in principle figure out how the world works.

You can hardly overstate how radical that is. Almost every previous human culture had assumed that ultimate knowledge was either inaccessible to humans or available only through divine intervention. The Presocratics — without ever quite saying it explicitly — assumed the opposite. They assumed the world was comprehensible and that we were the ones who could comprehend it.

That is not a method. That is an attitude. And it is the attitude on which everything later, including the scientific method itself, would eventually be built.

So when Waterfield says the Presocratics were not scientists, do not hear him as belittling them. Hear him as saying: they were something prior. They were the ones who decided that the project of science was worth attempting. Without that decision, no method.

Bringing Philosophy Down to Earth

Once you have decided that the human rational mind can comprehend the cosmos, a question becomes inevitable, and it is the most natural question in the world. Why stop at the cosmos?

If reason can chart the stars, can it not also chart us? Our laws, our customs, our sense of justice, our political arrangements — these too must have a structure beneath their surface. They too must be available to analysis.

The men who first asked this question seriously were not, as Cicero famously claimed, Socrates and his successors. They were the Sophists. And to understand why Cicero's line is misleading — and why Plato's smear has held up so well — you have to understand the world the Sophists were operating in.

Fifth-century Athens was, by any measure, an extraordinary place. The city had pulled off an astonishing experiment in direct, participatory democracy. The Assembly, the law courts, the civic life of the polis — these were not run by professionals on behalf of the population. They were run, in person, by ordinary male citizens. And in such a system, the ability to speak well — to persuade a crowd, to defend yourself in court, to argue a policy from the floor — was not a polite accomplishment. It was the very mechanism of power.

Athenian democracy thus created, almost overnight, an enormous demand for a kind of training that traditional Greek education simply did not offer. Wealthy young men needed to learn rhetoric, argumentation, the analysis of language, the mechanics of persuasion. Where was this taught? It wasn't — until the Sophists arrived to fill the gap.

The Sophists were itinerant professionals. They moved between cities, gave public lectures, took on private pupils, and accepted fees for their work. Plato found their commercialism distasteful, but the alternative was a society in which only the inherited aristocracy had access to political skill. The Sophists, in effect, opened the toolkit of democratic citizenship to anyone who could pay. Rhetoric, as Waterfield observes in passing, was then exactly what it remains today: the practical face of the right to free speech and a fair trial. It is not a luxury. It is the operating system of any society in which ordinary people are expected to argue their own case.

But the Sophists were not only rhetoricians. They were the first thinkers in the Greek tradition to take human matters — morality, law, custom, religion, language, education — as a philosophical subject in its own right. They asked whether justice was natural or merely conventional. They asked whether the gods were objective realities or human constructs. They asked whether moral rules were the same in every society, or whether they varied by culture. These are not the small questions of a mercenary class. These are the founding questions of moral and political philosophy, and they belong to the Sophists.

This is why Waterfield reverses Cicero's famous line. Cicero said it was Socrates who called philosophy down from the heavens — who turned its gaze from the cosmos to the human world. But the cosmos-to-human turn had already happened. The Sophists had already done the earthing. Socrates' contribution was different and, in its way, more disruptive: he wiped the slate clean. He came in after the Sophists had opened up moral and political inquiry, and he asked the harder, prior question — how do we know any of this in the first place? He did not invent humanism in philosophy. He invented the demand for foundations.

There is a final point here, and it is critical for the deeper reading you will do later. Waterfield notes — and this surprised me — that strict, step-by-step deductive argumentation does not appear among the Presocratics in any rigorous form until Parmenides. Earlier thinkers stated their visions; Parmenides was the first who tried to prove his. He is, in effect, the man who introduces logic as a method into Western philosophy. Hold onto that. When you read Parmenides, you should not only ask what he says — that reality is one, unchanging, indivisible. You should ask how he says it. Because the how is what Plato is going to inherit, polish, and weaponise. Parmenides's argumentative style is the actual seed from which Platonic dialectic eventually grows.

The New Gods of Consensus Reality

So we come to the question that closes Waterfield's Introduction and that he rightly treats as the most important one of all. Why bother with these thinkers today?

Their physics is wrong. Their cosmology is wrong. The earth is not a flat disk floating on water; the elements are not four; atoms are not indivisible; the heavens are not a series of crystalline spheres. By the standards of any modern science class, the Presocratics are obsolete. So why read them?

Waterfield's answer comes through a story.

Plutarch, writing several centuries after the events he describes, tells of a ship sailing past a small island in the Greek seas. As the ship drifts, the helmsman, a man named Thamus, hears a voice calling out from the shore. The voice gives him a strange instruction: when he reaches a certain harbour, he is to announce to those on the bank that Great Pan is dead. Thamus, when he reaches the place, does as he was told. He calls out the message. And from the unseen world along the shore there rises an enormous chorus of lamentation and confusion — a wailing of beings whose existence the new world will no longer admit.

Pan is the god of wild places, of irrational panic, of fertility and shaggy laughter, of the parts of human life that cannot be reduced to a clean argument. His death is a metaphor — and Plutarch's contemporaries certainly read it that way — for the passing of an entire mode of being. The disorderly old gods, the ones who could not be domesticated by reason, are leaving. The world is being sterilised. Logos has won.

This is exactly the story Schiller would tell again, eighteen centuries later, in the poem Waterfield used to open the Introduction. The same lament. The same supposed loss. Logos triumphs and mythos dies; we keep the equations and lose the gods.

And here, in his closing pages, Waterfield does something I find genuinely brilliant. He turns the whole story upside down.

Did logos really kill mythos? Or did we simply build a new myth and call it something else?

What is a myth? Strip the word of its modern, dismissive sense — the sense in which "myth" just means "false story". A myth, more carefully understood, is a framework: a structure of explanation that a community uses to make the world coherent, to share meaning, to know which questions are worth asking and which answers count. By that definition, modern science is unmistakably a myth. It is the framework through which our civilisation makes sense of reality, agrees on what is real, and decides which kinds of explanation are admissible. We are not a less mythological people than the Greeks. We are a differently mythological people. We have not banished the gods. We have merely, as Waterfield puts it with mischievous precision, birthed a new generation of scientific gods, and we worship them with a piety the Olympians would have envied.

This claim deserves to be taken seriously, because it is doing two things at once. It is not a piece of relativism that says science is no better than astrology. Science demonstrably works. The point is more subtle: science is doing the same job in our culture that the older mythologies did in theirs. It tells us what is real and what is not. It tells us which kinds of knowing count. It produces consensus. The fact that it does this job exceptionally well does not change the fact that it is doing the job at all.

And this brings Waterfield to the deepest claim in the Introduction, the one I want to leave you with.

Each of us, he writes, is a bundle of rational and irrational impulses. The two cannot be cleanly separated, and the attempt to separate them is doomed — as doomed as the attempt to separate the science of the Presocratics from their mysticism. They were both at once, and so are we. A human being is not a thinking machine with some unfortunate emotional weather around it. A human being is a creature in whom logic and intuition, argument and image, logos and mythos, are interwoven all the way down. To amputate either side in the name of the other is to perform a kind of self-mutilation. Pure rationality is a fiction, and it is not even an admirable fiction; it is a dangerous one.

This is why we read the first philosophers, even though their conclusions are wrong. They are wrong in interesting ways — ways that show us a mind in the process of doing both things at once, reasoning and dreaming, calculating and prophesying, looking at the world and looking through it. Empedocles the natural philosopher and Empedocles the shaman are the same man, and that is not a contradiction to be resolved. It is a model to learn from.

The Lens I Will Carry Forward

When I now turn to the fragments of Parmenides and Heraclitus, and from them to Plato, and eventually to Nietzsche — who admired Heraclitus precisely because Heraclitus refused the static logos that Plato was about to enthrone — I will be carrying Waterfield's Introduction with me as a set of warnings and a set of permissions.

The warnings: do not trust the framing. Aristotle is reading the Presocratics through Aristotle. Plato is reading the Sophists through his loyalty to Socrates. The textbook is reading all of them through a story of progress that the evidence does not actually support. Read with suspicion of every neat narrative.

The permissions: do not feel obliged to choose between the rational and the mystical, the analytical and the imaginative, the philosopher and the poet. The first thinkers did not choose, and their refusal to choose is not a sign of their primitiveness. It is a sign of their honesty about what a human mind actually is.

Schiller was wrong. The old gods did not die. They went into hiding inside our equations, inside our political ideologies, inside our technologies, inside the very stories we tell ourselves about what we are. The history of philosophy is not the march of logos over the corpse of mythos. It is, as Homer already knew before any of this began, a long dance between them — a dance in which the old gods, in some disguise or other, never truly leave the floor.

That is the lens. And with it, we can finally begin to read.
